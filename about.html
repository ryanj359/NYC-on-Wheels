About

New York City is no stranger to innovative food establishments, with mobile vendors being one of the most historic. From the early pushcarts to today's food trucks, the history of street vending runs deep in the city.

In the 1800s, immigrant communities introduced pushcarts. Mainly in the Lower East Side of Manhattan, they would sell food amongst each other, or to workers in the area who either couldn't afford to eat out or didn't have the time to cook at home. This also provided these immigrants with a chance to make money and be included in the city's economy.

In the early 2000s, food trucks made a comeback; these trucks began selling their takes on traditional street food, as well as new items.

The vendors of today face many challenges, one of which is getting permits. Obtaining a permit requires a great deal of patience; the city only offers a limited amount of permits each year, so even after submitting applications, completing courses, and paying fees, you still are not guaranteed a permit right away. Kelebohile Nkhereanye, a seasoned street vendor and activist, strongly believes that vendors need and deserve their voices heard. The street food culture in New York City today is a myriad of cuisines from all over, which is a very accurate reflection of the diverse culture in the city. Vendors continue to play a crucial role in providing affordable and diverse food options, contributing to the city's vibrant food scene. But, they also face ongoing challenges, emphasizing the need for continued advocacy and support.

The history of mobile food vendors in New York City is evidence of the city's immigrant heritage and entrepreneurial spirit. As the city continues to evolve and grow, so will its street food culture.
